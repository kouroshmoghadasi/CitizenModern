    • La Colombie-Britannique (C.-B.), sur la côte Pacifique, est la province la plus occidentale du Canada, avec une population de quatre millions d’habitants.

    • Environ la moitié des biens produits en C.-B. sont des produits forestiers, dont bois d’œuvre, papier journal et pâtes et papiers—l’industrie forestière la plus précieuse au Canada. La C.-B. est aussi connue pour les mines, la pêche et les vergers et la viticulture de la vallée de l’Okanagan.

    Territoires du Nord

    • Les Territoires du Nord-Ouest, le Nunavut et le Yukon représentent un tiers du territoire canadien mais ne comptent qu’environ 100 000 habitants.

    • Le Yukon détient le record de la température la plus froide jamais enregistrée au Canada : −63 °C.

    Mount Logan

    • Mount Logan, in the Yukon, is Canada’s highest peak. It is named for Sir William Logan, a world-famous geologist born in Montreal in 1798 to Scottish immigrant parents.
    • Logan founded and directed the Geological Survey of Canada from 1842 to 1869 and is counted among Canada’s greatest scientists.

    NWT (Northwest Territories)

    • Formed in 1870 from Rupert’s Land and the North-Western Territory.
    • Capital Yellowknife (population about 20,000), known as the “diamond capital of North America.”
    • More than half the population is Aboriginal (Dene, Inuit and Métis).
    • The Mackenzie River, about 4,200 km long, is the second-longest river system in North America after the Mississippi and drains an area of about 1.8 million square kilometres.

    Nunavut

    • Nunavut means “our land” in Inuktitut. Established in 1999 from the eastern Northwest Territories, including the former District of Keewatin.
    • Capital Iqaluit (formerly Frobisher Bay), named for English explorer Martin Frobisher, who explored the Arctic for Queen Elizabeth I in 1576.
    • A 19-member Legislative Assembly chooses a premier and ministers by consensus.
    • The population is about 85% Inuit. Inuktitut is an official language and the primary language taught in schools.

    • Part of the Canadian Forces Reserves (militia); they address security and sovereignty challenges in harsh, isolated northern regions.
    • Using indigenous knowledge, they travel by snowmobile in winter and all-terrain vehicles in summer from Resolute toward the Magnetic North Pole.
    • The caribou (reindeer) is popular game for hunters and a symbol of Canada’s North.
